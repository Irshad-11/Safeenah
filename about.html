01   What is Safeenah
مَثَلُ أَهْلِ بَيْتِي فِيكُمْ كَمَثَلِ سَفِينَةِ نُوحٍ — مَنْ رَكِبَهَا نَجَا وَمَنْ تَخَلَّفَ عَنْهَا غَرِقَ
"The example of my Ahlul Bayt among you is like Noah's Ark — whoever boards it is saved, and whoever stays behind is drowned."
— Hadith al-Safeenah  ·  Al-Mustadrak, Vol. 3  ·  Narrated by Abu Dharr al-Ghifari

Safeenah is a personal research archive — a place where Islamic historical records are documented with care, sourced transparently, and presented without hiding inconvenient truths. The name was chosen deliberately: not as a sectarian statement, but as a challenge to the reader. Board the ship. Look at the primary sources. Judge for yourself.

This project exists in the gap between what we have been taught and what is actually recorded. Islam's history is vast, rich, and at times contested. Safeenah does not pretend otherwise.

02   The Story Behind This

A Student Who Saw a Contradiction

The Spark
I am Irshad Hossain, a Software Engineering student from Bangladesh. My field is code — but my curiosity pulled me into Islamic history in a way I did not expect. It started watching debates between Sunni and Shia scholars online. What disturbed me was not the disagreement. It was something simpler: the Shia side would cite events from Sunni hadith collections — books we claim as our own — and I had never heard of those events. Not once.
The Question
How could an event be recorded in Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Musnad Ahmad — the most trusted Sunni hadith collections — and yet be completely absent from the education of a practicing Sunni Muslim? The answer, I began to understand, is not conspiracy. It is selective emphasis. What a school of thought focuses on shapes what its followers know. What it downplays, slowly disappears from living memory.
The Contradiction
The Prophet ﷺ, across his entire life, placed the Ahlul Bayt — his household — at the highest level of importance. Hadith al-Thaqalayn, Hadith al-Safeenah, Hadith al-Manzila, the events at Ghadir Khum — these are not weak narrations tucked away in obscure manuscripts. They are in our books. Yet the mainstream Sunni tradition has gradually, generation by generation, reduced the weight given to the Ahlul Bayt to the point where their centrality in the Prophetic message feels almost invisible.
The Archive
I am not a scholar. I am not a theologian. I am someone who started writing things down — documenting events, recording hadith with their full chains, noting which books they appear in, presenting what different scholarly traditions say about them. Safeenah is that notebook, made public. It is not a platform for any single school of thought. It is an invitation to look at the primary sources yourself and judge through the frame of Al-Quran and the Ahlul Bayt.

03   Methodology

How Records Are Documented

📚
Primary Sources First
Every hadith and event is traced to its earliest recorded source — classical hadith collections, historical texts like Tarikh al-Tabari, and canonical works from both traditions.
⚖️
Multiple Perspectives
Where Sunni and Shia scholarship differ in interpretation, both views are documented side by side. The reader decides — Safeenah does not decide for you.
🔗
Full Chain of Narrators
Each hadith includes its chain (isnad) with narrator reliability noted. Weak, strong, disputed — all of this is shown transparently, not hidden to protect a narrative.
🚫
No Hidden Agenda
This is a personal research archive, not a propaganda platform. Records that challenge the mainstream Sunni position are documented because they exist — not to attack anyone's faith.

04   Why Only a Frontend?

Accessibility Before Infrastructure

This is an honest question that deserves an honest answer. Safeenah is currently a pure frontend archive — HTML, CSS, and JSON files with no backend server, no database, no login system. This was a deliberate choice made under real constraints.

First: budget. Running a backend with a database has costs. As a student, those costs are not sustainable. A static site can be hosted for free, forked on GitHub, and accessed by anyone anywhere in the world without a monthly bill.

Second: portability and preservation. Static JSON files can be downloaded, backed up, and archived by anyone. If this site disappears tomorrow, the data does not have to. Anyone can take the JSON files and rebuild. Knowledge should not be locked behind a server that might go offline.

Third: speed of access. At this stage of the project, getting the records documented and visible matters more than building infrastructure. Perfect is the enemy of present.

There is a longer-term possibility of merging this data into a larger project — potentially Tarikh al-Islam — but nothing is decided. For now, Safeenah is exactly what it needs to be: a ship, not a harbour.

Open data: All records in this archive are stored as plain JSON files. You can view, download, and use them freely. Knowledge belongs to everyone.
